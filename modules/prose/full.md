# Skill — prose

## What this skill is for

Prose carries arguments. Thin prose, thin argument; mannered prose, the
argument buried; hedging prose, a claim the reader does not trust. This
is the workspace's prose layer — applied anywhere the page itself is the
load-bearing surface: essays in TheBookOfSol, ESSENCE-class documents,
audit reports, a substack post, the prose inside a `skills.md`, a
covering letter.

The register: **wise and controlled, not pompous; subtle and
knowledgeable; not flashy, but deeply shocking.** The shock comes from
compression and accuracy, never from ornament. TheBookOfSol is the
reference — its essays are the workspace's most-developed prose; its
`AGENTS.md` holds project-specific rules (Sanskrit-first quote blocks,
"chloride of sodium," "Earth is not a planet"). This skill extracts what
generalises.

It does not apply to identifiers, code comments, or commit messages —
see `skills/naming.md` and `skills/jj.md`.

## The criterion

Two laws upstream of every concrete move.

### I. The iceberg — show by what you omit

> "If a writer of prose knows enough about what he is writing about he
> may omit things that he knows and the reader, if the writer is writing
> truly enough, will have a feeling of those things as strongly as
> though the writer had stated them. The dignity of movement of an
> iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water. A writer
> who omits things because he does not know them only makes hollow
> places in his writing."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *Death in the Afternoon*, Ch. 16

The dignity is the test. A dignified passage rests on seven-eighths the
reader does not see; a hollow one omits what the writer never knew. The
discipline is not to write less — it is to know more, then choose what
to omit. The companion law, from *A Moveable Feast*: "Write the truest
sentence that you know," and cut every scrollwork or ornament around it.
The first true simple declarative sentence is the iceberg's tip;
everything else earns its place by deepening it, or gets cut.

### II. Let the elders carry — sources as load-bearing structure

The most reliable way to sound old is to bring the old voices forward
and let them speak. The prose around a primary source is scaffolding;
the source is the load-bearing surface. Speaking *over* a source —
paraphrasing it, summarising what it says — concedes the territory it
already holds. When multiple traditions converge on a point, name them
all: the plurality of voices is the evidence that the thing has been
seen, and is what makes a claim classical.

Default ratio: one substantive primary-source block per 150–300 words,
the surrounding prose doing only framing. A passage that feels
unsupported usually needed a quote three lines earlier; one that feels
overstuffed usually had two quotes that should have been one. If no
primary source exists, write the claim cleanly on its own merits — never
fake one with "studies have shown" or "many believe." Name the witness
or own the unbacked claim.

## Voice

### Declarative, present, unhedged

The prose stands in the present tense, in the voice of the finished
work. Hedges ("perhaps," "it could be argued that," "one might say") are
evidence the claim is not yet earned — the writer asking the reader to
share the doubt instead of doing the work that removes it. Either the
claim is supported and stated, or it is struck. The hedge is the worst
of both: it costs trust without protecting the writer, because the claim
is still there underneath.

The first person appears decisively or not at all. When it appears it
owns the position and the body — *"I am glad I ate the Walmart butter.
It made me a more formidable warrior."* Sustained "I think," "I believe"
is weaker than the same prose with the *I* removed; the claim stands or
falls on what it asserts, not whose head it sits inside. Use the *I*
when its presence is part of the meaning; otherwise drop it.

### Wise, not pompous

The hardest discipline of the register. Wisdom and pomposity share
surfaces — present tense, long quotation, the aphoristic close. Two
tests distinguish them.

**Does the sentence add or perform?** A wise sentence hands the reader
an instrument they did not bring in — *"Nature loves to hide."* A
pompous one hands them the writer's performance — *"In the rich tapestry
of natural mystery, one observes that the hidden is the ground of the
revealed."* Strike every sentence whose work is to be admired.

**Does the diction belong to the subject or to a costume?** A wise
sentence borrows its diction from the subject it names — craft language
for craft, body for body: *"The smith works in a particular mood. The
mood is steady."* A pompous one borrows from the *idea of being
literary*, the elevated word imported because elevation is the effect:
*"The artisan labors in a particular humour."* Same content, costumed.
Use the word that names the thing: *"smith"* not *"artisan"*; *"fire"*
not *"flame"*.

## Stake — the weight of what the prose is about

Prose carries the weight of its subject. Hemingway's observational form
holds because his subjects carry weight: death, war, sickness,
bullfighting. The form needs the weight to land.

The opening tests this most acutely. For an **argument**, the stake
lives in the claim — the cold open is the position stated as flatly as
it can be: *"The world is a torus."* / *"The law perverted!"* For a
**narrative**, the stake lives in the action — the opening is the scene
where the action is about to begin (Hemingway's *Indian Camp* at the
lake shore at dawn). The two cold opens look alike — declarative,
present, no meta-prose — but the argumentative opening **states** while
the narrative opening **shows**.

Find the stake before drafting. What is the argument's cost? What does
the position correct? What happens to a body, a country, a sentence when
the contrary is followed? Open on that. A cold open on a domestic scene
that disputes nothing has begun in decoration, however carefully
arranged — *"Rice and water in a clay pot over coals."* The form is
correct, the stake absent. Find the cost the argument corrects and open
there instead.

## Diction

### The lexicon is the argument

Vocabulary is diagnostic. When the prevailing word for a thing is
inherited from a frame the argument is contesting, that word is part of
what the argument has to defeat. TheBookOfSol writes *"chloride of
sodium"* for *"sodium chloride"* because the chloride anion is the toxic
agent and the standard construction obscures it; from there the rest of
the lexicon — *chloridic*, *chloridism*, *chlorinated diet* — unfolds
with the argument. Choose the word that names the agent, then use it
consistently. A piece that keeps the wrong vocabulary cannot be polished
into the right position.

### Technical terms — gloss once

Articles are read by non-experts. Sanskrit terms, classical text names,
and physiological concepts carry a parenthetical or inline English gloss
on first appearance in each article; subsequent uses drop it. Never
assume the reader has met *agni*, *ojas*, *snigdha*, *mitāhāra*, *Caraka
Saṃhitā* before.

## Opening

Drop the reader into the argument. Do not announce what the piece will
do, summarise its sections, or introduce the cast — the first sentence
is part of the argument, not a preface to it. Delete on sight: "This
essay does two things…", "In what follows, we will see…", "A few
concepts are worth introducing at the outset…", "Notice that…", "Observe
how…". Opening with meta-prose is the most reliable signal of an author
not yet sure of the position. Strike it; commit or cut.

The opening particular, when used, is the **contested particular** — the
specific thing the argument will dispute or defend, carrying the stake.

**Concrete to abstract.** Open in particulars; rise into doctrine. The
reverse leaves the reader guessing what the abstraction is for until the
example arrives. Reference move — *Apathya*: open with a specific recipe
half the internet considers Ayurvedic; show the eleven ingredients on
the plate; then bring the classical doctrine that disqualifies it clause
by clause. The reader has already visualised the failure before the
doctrine names it.

## Working with sources

When a primary source exists — a classical text, a study, a contemporary
statement, a numerical record — let it carry the argument:

1. One or two paragraphs of plain orientation.
2. The primary-source quote block.
3. Minimal commentary — what the quote established.
4. The next quote, or the next move.

### One image, held

A quote-dense essay needs one load-bearing image at its centre — the
refiner's silver, the chloride dissolving the membrane, the torus
inhaling and exhaling. Every quote selected, every section opened, every
sentence deepens *that* image.

The drift to avoid: a strong quote arrives in a different metaphor and
the writer brings it in *because* it is strong. Water-as-soft-overcoming-
hard, names-must-be-rectified, the householder-supports-all-*āśramas* —
each is a fine anchor for its own essay; stacked in one article they
braid many essays into one and hold to none. Pick the image before
drafting; cut every quote that does not deepen it; if a cut quote is too
strong to lose, save it for the article it belongs to. A single-image
essay reads as one thing; a braided essay reads as a tour.

## Quote framing — modes

When a primary source enters the prose, the writer chooses how to bring
it on. Four modes are available; one is forbidden.

**Mode 1 — Epigraph.** Quote stands alone, attribution at the end, no
introducer. The prose continues afterward, addressing what the quote
raised. Hemingway's preferred mode (the Ecclesiastes of *The Sun Also
Rises*); the cold-open-to-quote rhythm of `The_Toroidal_Heart`.

**Mode 2 — Colon-attribution.** Author's name + colon, then the quote.
Minimum framing, useful when the reader already knows the author:
*"Heraclitus: 'Nature loves to hide.'"* Identificational only, never
evaluative.

**Mode 3 — Inline embedding.** The quote sits grammatically inside the
writer's sentence: *"What Heraclitus called 'an everliving fire' the
Vedic tradition called* agni*."* Useful when the quote is short or
doctrinal and the sentence wraps it cleanly.

**Mode 4 — Substantive participial.** Author's name + a participial
phrase that adds real fact (place, time, addressee, what the quote is
about to do), then the quote: *"Marcus Aurelius, writing for himself in
Pannonia between campaigns, translated the cosmic measure into a rule
for his own day:"* Each clause earns its place by adding fact the reader
needs — audience, place, function. Substance, not performance.

**Forbidden — the hype-up.** Author's name + an evaluative phrase about
the quote: *"X said it cleanest:"*, *"Y put it best:"*, *"As Z
observed:"*, *"W had the right of it when he wrote:"*. The phrase
performs the writer's judgment about the quote rather than orienting the
reader to its substance.

Default to Mode 1 or Mode 4; Modes 2 and 3 are tactical. When in doubt,
drop the introducer and let the quote stand. The test: *does the
introducer add a fact the reader doesn't have, or tell the reader how to
feel about the quote?* Facts stay; feelings get cut.

## The sentence

**Rhythm.** Sentences vary in length but cluster short. The aphoristic
register sits inside the prose: *"Anna is the floor. Fruit is the broom.
A broom is useful. But no one lives on a broom."* Four short sentences,
each closing a thought, the cumulative movement the claim. Long
sentences are admitted when they earn the breath — a list that must stay
together, a clause whose internal structure is the point — never as
default.

Em-dashes mark genuine asides or rhythmic breaks, not default emphasis.
A paragraph with three or more is almost always restructurable into
cleaner syntax; keep density below roughly five per 500 words. Use
straight quotes (`"` `'`) — curly quotes are a Word / ChatGPT export
artefact.

**Lists as drumbeat.** When the catalogue *is* the argument, write it in
full — the cumulative weight of items recited, named, spelled out is
what carries the claim no single item could. Reference move — *The
Chloride Indictment*: Bright, Widal, Achard, Digne, Hoelzel, Kempner,
Dahl, DASH-Sodium, SSaSS, Yanomami. By the end of the recital the reader
has *felt* the consensus, not been told about it. The list earns its
length only when each item is load-bearing; three weak items are worse
than the strongest one alone.

**The closer — aphoristic, not restatement.** End on a brief
declarative, sometimes one line. The closer distils the argument the
prose has earned; it does not summarise the argument the prose just
made. Reference closers: *"Remove chloride. Life resumes."* /
*"No one lives on a broom."* / *"The field is one. The Lights are two.
The breath is one. The body is at home."* Restatement ("In summary, the
argument has shown that…") flattens what the prose built. Cut it.

## The reference shelf

The working shelf — the voices the writer keeps near the desk, opens at
random, copies a sentence from before writing his own. Each entry names
*what that author teaches* and shows one passage that demonstrates the
lesson; the library carries the full corpus of each.

### Heraclitus — the gnomic fragment

Compression to seven words; paradox as the form of truth; nature spoken
in koan. What a sentence sounds like when nothing more can be removed.

> "Nature loves to hide."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment X (Kahn)

> "The way up and down is one and the same."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment CIII (Kahn)

When the prose feels too explanatory, find the seven-word version.

### Plutarch — the moral seeing

The opening that drops into a letter to a friend, the simile drawn from
craft, the patient circling of a single ethical question. The *Moralia*
are essays in the original sense — a question turned over until its shape
comes clear.

> "As the bees draw honey from the thyme, which is a most unsavory and
> dry herb, extract something that is convenient and useful even from
> the most bitter afflictions."\
> — Plutarch, *Moralia*, "Of the Tranquillity of the Mind"

When an argument needs to circle, follow Plutarch.

### Cicero — the address as scaffolding

The letter to a son. The pedagogical voice that does not condescend
because it loves the addressee.

> "No phase of life, whether public or private, whether in business or
> in the home… can be without its moral duty."\
> — Cicero, *De Officiis* I.4

When the piece is teaching, address the reader as Cicero addresses
Marcus — directly, by name if there is one, assuming they have already
begun.

### Marcus Aurelius — the morning resolve

The journal not written for an audience. The numbered self-contained
meditation. The voice that addresses itself in the second person —
*thou*.

> "In the morning when thou findest thyself unwilling to rise, consider
> with thyself presently, it is to go about a man's work that I am
> stirred up."\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* V.1

When the piece needs interior force, write toward the second person that
addresses the self. The reader overhears.

### Hemingway — the iceberg in practice

Short declarative sentences; no interior monologue where action will do;
the horror or grace in what the sentence does not say.

> "They shot the six cabinet ministers at half-past six in the morning
> against the wall of a hospital. There were pools of water in the
> courtyard. There were wet dead leaves on the paving of the courtyard.
> It rained hard. All the shutters of the hospital were nailed shut. One
> of the ministers was sick with typhoid. Two soldiers carried him
> downstairs and out into the rain. They tried to hold him up against
> the wall but he sat down in a puddle of water."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *In Our Time*, Chapter 6

No commentary. The pity is in the puddle. When a passage carries weight
that should not be named directly, describe what is in front of the
narrator and trust the reader to feel what is not named.

### Blake — the proverb as scripture

The aphorism that inverts what was assumed; the diction of the prophet
with no patience for the temple. The short declarative sentence as a
religious form.

> "The road of excess leads to the palace of wisdom."\
> — William Blake, *The Marriage of Heaven and Hell*

When a section needs a closer that does not summarise, find the Blakean
proverb — declarative, image-bearing, paradoxical enough to slow the
reader down.

### Bastiat — the satire whose target shows itself

The mock-petition, the mock-dialogue, the absurd premise extended with
formal courtesy until it dismantles itself. Political prose with the
patience of a chess player.

> "We are suffering from the intolerable competition of a foreign rival,
> placed, it would seem, in a condition so far superior to ours for the
> production of light… This rival, who is no other than the Sun, wages
> war to the knife against us."\
> — Frédéric Bastiat, *Economic Sophisms*, "Petition of the
> Manufacturers of Candles"

When the target is so absurd that direct attack would be heavier than
the target, adopt the absurd position and extend its logic with courtesy
until it falls.

### Ptolemy — the technical voice that names things

When the subject is technical — astronomy, medicine, a working
framework — the *Tetrabiblos* register: precise vocabulary, relational
definitions, the calm of a hand that has drawn the diagram many times.
No apology for the technicality. Use the domain's own words without
translation; the reader who needs the gloss has it once at first
appearance.

### Vedic and Upaniṣadic — the sūtra

Compression to the single syllable; the aphoristic verse polished by
twenty centuries of repetition; the line that does not explain itself
because it does not need to.

> **एकं सद् विप्रा बहुधा वदन्ति।**\
> *ekaṃ sad viprā bahudhā vadanti.*
>
> "Truth is one; the sages call it by many names."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 1.164.46

> **तत्त्वमसि।** *tat tvam asi.* — "Thou art that." — *Chāndogya
> Upaniṣad* 6.8.7

When the argument arrives where the doctrine is already crystallised, the
verse speaks; the prose around it is preparation.

### Carlos Castaneda — the metaphor that holds the cosmos

Philosophy fully entered into image — at once ethnographic-flat and
metaphysically unflinching. The metaphor is the cognitive instrument,
never decorative: death as a hunter at one's left, inner silence as the
mortar between bricks.

> "Death is the only wise adviser that we have. Whenever you feel… that
> everything is going wrong and you're about to be annihilated, turn to
> your death and ask if that is so. Your death will tell you, 'I haven't
> touched you yet.'"\
> — Carlos Castaneda, *Journey to Ixtlan*, "Death is an Adviser"

When a concept is more cleanly carried by an image than an argument, hand
it the image. Castanedan metaphor is the test that a position has been
thought through far enough to have a *shape*.

### Juan Arnau — the contemporary wisdom-prose bridge

The living voice closest to the target register: an astrophysicist and
Sanskritist who binds philosophy of science to the Vedic and Greek
traditions without academic apology — gracile, declarative, stating the
position before defending it.

> "El progreso ha sido hasta ahora ciego. No sabe adónde va. Es hora de
> frenar, de reconducir."\
> — Juan Arnau, *La fuga de dios* (2023)

Arnau's lesson, in English: state the position; name the witnesses
(philosophers of science, ancient commentators, modern physicists) on
the same page; move between traditions without apology. The bridge from
Whitehead to the Upaniṣads is the argument, not ornament.

### Rudhyar — the cosmological-poetic register

When the writing aims at the level of the cosmos itself: personalising
and collectivising forces, systole and diastole, the cycle named in
plain English without occultist mystique or scientific distancing.

> "Reality has a rhythmic heart. The systole and diastole of that heart
> create these beats of becoming which are birth and death, winter and
> summer, increase of light and crescendos of darkness."\
> — Dane Rudhyar, *The Pulse of Life*

When the field is cosmic — cycles, scales, the body in the year, the year
in the cosmos — Rudhyar names the pulse without flattening it or dressing
it up.

## What to delete on sight

### Negative-contrast (the LLM tic)

*"X is not Y. It is Z."* The signature ChatGPT move — building a positive
claim by first denying an alternative. Mechanical, tedious in repetition,
adds no information. State the positive directly. Variants: *"X is not Y;
it is Z."* / *"Not merely X, but Y."* / *"Not A. Not B. Not C. But D."*
(triple-fragment hammer) / a standalone *"This is not [thing]. It is
[other thing]."* If the dismissed alternative carries information (a
misreading the piece corrects), absorb it as a trailing concession — *"X
is Z, not Y"* — sparingly, never as default. If a piece uses the pattern
more than ~3 times, it has become the default rhythm and needs a full
pass.

### Filler phrases

- "it is worth noting," "it is important to note," "it should be
  remembered"
- "notably," "crucially," "indeed," "moreover," "furthermore" (as
  paragraph openers)
- "truly," "fundamentally," "literally" (in non-literal contexts)
- "delves into," "unpacks," "navigates the complexities of," "in the
  realm of," "rich tapestry," "at the intersection of"
- "the fact that…" (almost always compressible)

### Meta-prose

No announcing the structure of the piece. Do not surface the project's
own writing conventions inside an article either — vocabulary swaps,
em-dash density, the not-X-but-Y rule, curly-vs-straight quotes. Those
are workshop discipline, invisible to the reader; an article that points
at them reads as the project talking about itself.

### Stage-setting and the quote hype-up

*"Notice that…", "Observe how…", "Note that…"* — do not cue the reader to
attend; let the sentence merit attention on its own. The same tic in
attribution costume — *"X said it cleanest:"*, *"As Z observed:"*, *"X
said it for the first time"* (where "first" is evaluative, not
historical) — performs the writer's judgment about the quote. Either drop
the introducer (Mode 1) or replace the evaluation with a substantive
participial that adds context the reader needs (Mode 4).

## What it sounds like — and what it does not

Three passages in the register:

> "Maera lay still, his head on his arms, his face in the sand. He felt
> warm and sticky from the bleeding. Each time he felt the horn coming…
> Once the horn went all the way through him and he felt it go into the
> sand."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *In Our Time*, Chapter 16

Observation as judgment withheld. The pity emerges from the absence of
it.

> "The Bhagavad Gītā requires sāttvic food to be *snigdha* — unctuous,
> oily, lubricating — and *sthira* — stable, substantial, enduring. Ghee
> is both. Anna cooked with ghee is both. A diet without ghee fails these
> requirements structurally, regardless of what else it contains."\
> — TheBookOfSol, *The Ambrosian Diet*

Quote-load and aphoristic close: the doctrine, the inference, the
negation, the verdict, no throat-clearing between them.

Three passages of what the register *will not* produce, each to feel for
and rewrite away from:

- **Academic distancing.** *"Many scholars have argued that Heraclitus's
  view of fire may be interpreted as a kind of cosmic principle, though
  some recent commentators have raised objections."* Strike the
  scholars; quote Heraclitus directly.
- **Journalistic crescendo.** *"In a world where the speed of change is
  accelerating… perhaps the ancient wisdom of the Stoics offers a
  surprising remedy."* Strike the world-on-fire framing; state the Stoic
  position; trust the reader to recognise the application.
- **The mystical drape.** *"Deep within the sacred geometries of the
  cosmic torus, the soul whispers its ancient knowing to the attuned
  heart."* Strike "deep within," "sacred," "whispers," "ancient
  knowing." Find the structural claim underneath; if there is one, write
  *that*; if not, delete the sentence.

The negative test, in one line: *would Heraclitus or Hemingway have let
this sentence stand?* If no, rewrite it.

## When the prose feels off

Each item below is a *signal*, not a sin. Notice it; decide the
underlying problem; fix the underlying problem.

- **A sentence the reader has to re-read.** The structure is not yet
  right. Find the structure that parses on the first read.
- **A claim that needs three qualifiers to be defensible.** Either the
  qualifiers belong inside the claim, or the claim is smaller than
  written. Shrink the claim or kill the qualifiers.
- **A passive construction hiding the actor.** Name the actor. *"The
  river carried the silt"* over *"the silt was carried."*
- **Repetition where one good word would do.** Find the word.
- **A paragraph of em-dashes.** The dashes are doing what syntax should.
- **A paragraph that paraphrases a primary source you have at hand.**
  Bring the source forward, frame it, let it speak.
- **An opening that introduces what the piece will do.** Cut it; start
  with the substance.
- **A closing that summarises what the piece just did.** Cut it; let the
  closer be the aphoristic distillate.
- **A sentence whose work is to be admired.** Strike it; replace with one
  whose work is to *add*.
- **Diction borrowed from the idea of being literary, not the subject.**
  Replace with the word that names the thing.

## When the writing arrives at its own form

A high-register piece does not begin in this register. It begins as a
clear claim, accumulates source-witnesses, and gradually finds the image
and rhythm that hold it together. The register is the result of revision,
not of first drafting.

Drafting discipline is the simpler one: declarative, present-tense,
source-anchored, image-disciplined. Revision discipline is this skill in
full: read the draft against the reference shelf, and where the prose
feels heavier than Heraclitus, lighter than Hemingway, dimmer than Blake,
find the sentence that broke the register and rewrite from the noun.

If it sounds like Hemingway translating Marcus Aurelius for Juan Arnau to
read, the register is right.
